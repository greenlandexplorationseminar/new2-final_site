Landslide Science

Understanding the geological processes and mechanisms behind landslide phenomena

Tsunamis

Landslides, by themselves, normally just impact the area immediately around where the event occurs. However, landslides that end up in bodies of water frequently create tsunamis. Tsunamis can travel further than landslides, and have the potential to cause major destruction of property or even loss of life. They also travel quickly. It can be a matter of minutes between when a landslide hits the water and when a nearby village sees huge waves.

The size of tsunamis depends on the speed and energy the material from the landslide has when entering the water. This can depend on a number of factors. The steepness of a slope, resistance to movement provided to the ground, and mass of moving materials all impact how big a resulting tsunami will be.

Dip-Slope Failure

Dip-slope failure is a type of landslide that occurs when rock layers are inclined (dipping) in the same direction as the slope face. This geological configuration creates planes of weakness along which failure can occur. When the angle of the bedding planes is less steep than the slope itself, gravitational forces can cause large sections of rock to slide along these planes. Dip-slope failures are common in areas with sedimentary or metamorphic rocks and can involve massive volumes of material, sometimes extending deep into the mountainside.

Molard

A molard is a distinctive conical mound or debris pile that forms from the disintegration of frozen ground material after a landslide. When a landslide involves permafrost or ice-rich material, the subsequent thawing and breakdown of these frozen blocks creates characteristic cone-shaped deposits on the landslide runout area. Molards are important indicators of permafrost involvement in mass movements and are particularly common in Arctic and sub-Arctic regions like Greenland, where warming temperatures are causing increased permafrost degradation and contributing to slope instability.
